Aspirin (Acetylsalicylic Acid): Pharmacological Profile and Molecular Properties

SMILES: CC(=O)OC1=CC=CC=C1C(=O)O
Molecular Formula: C9H8O4
CAS Number: 50-78-2

1. PHYSICOCHEMICAL PROPERTIES
Aspirin has a molecular weight of 180.16 g/mol. The calculated LogP (octanol-water partition coefficient) is approximately 1.19, indicating moderate lipophilicity suitable for oral absorption. The topological polar surface area (TPSA) is 63.60 Angstrom squared, which falls well within the range for good oral bioavailability (TPSA < 140). Aspirin has 1 hydrogen bond donor (the carboxylic acid OH) and 4 hydrogen bond acceptors (the two carbonyl oxygens and the two ester/acid oxygens).

Lipinski's Rule of Five Assessment:
- MW = 180.16 (pass, < 500)
- LogP = 1.19 (pass, < 5)
- HBD = 1 (pass, <= 5)
- HBA = 4 (pass, <= 10)
Verdict: Aspirin passes all four Lipinski criteria, consistent with its established oral bioavailability.

The Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness (QED) for aspirin is approximately 0.55, which places it in the moderate drug-likeness range. This is influenced by its relatively low molecular complexity.

2. MECHANISM OF ACTION
Aspirin irreversibly inhibits cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1) and cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) enzymes by acetylating a serine residue (Ser530 in COX-1, Ser516 in COX-2) in the active site. This prevents the conversion of arachidonic acid to prostaglandin H2, the precursor of prostaglandins and thromboxane A2.

At low doses (75-100 mg/day), aspirin preferentially inhibits COX-1 in platelets, reducing thromboxane A2 production and thus inhibiting platelet aggregation. This forms the basis for its cardiovascular protective effects.

At higher doses (325-650 mg), aspirin inhibits both COX-1 and COX-2, providing analgesic and anti-inflammatory effects. The anti-inflammatory action is primarily mediated through COX-2 inhibition in tissue macrophages and other inflammatory cells.

3. PHARMACOKINETICS
Absorption: Rapidly absorbed from the stomach and upper small intestine. Peak plasma concentrations reached within 1-2 hours.
Distribution: Widely distributed; protein binding is concentration-dependent (80-90% at therapeutic concentrations).
Metabolism: Rapidly hydrolyzed to salicylic acid (active metabolite) by esterases in blood and tissues. Salicylic acid is further metabolized in the liver via conjugation with glycine and glucuronic acid.
Elimination: Renal excretion of metabolites. Half-life of aspirin is 15-20 minutes; half-life of salicylic acid is 2-3 hours at low doses but can extend to 15-30 hours at high doses due to saturable metabolism.

4. TOXICITY PROFILE
Aspirin does not contain known structural alerts for mutagenicity or carcinogenicity. It lacks reactive functional groups such as Michael acceptors, epoxides, acyl halides, or nitrogen mustards. It does not match PAINS (Pan-Assay Interference Compounds) patterns.

The primary toxicity concerns are:
- Gastrointestinal: Erosion of gastric mucosa due to local COX-1 inhibition, leading to ulceration and bleeding.
- Reye's Syndrome: Association with hepatic and cerebral damage in children with viral infections.
- Salicylism: At very high doses (>150 mg/kg), symptoms include tinnitus, hearing loss, nausea, and metabolic acidosis.

5. CLINICAL APPLICATIONS
- Cardiovascular prophylaxis (low-dose, 75-100 mg/day)
- Analgesic (moderate pain, 325-650 mg every 4-6 hours)
- Anti-inflammatory (rheumatic conditions, 3-6 g/day in divided doses)
- Antipyretic (fever reduction)
- Kawasaki disease (high-dose followed by low-dose regimen)
- Colorectal cancer chemoprevention (emerging evidence, 75-325 mg/day)

6. STRUCTURE-ACTIVITY RELATIONSHIPS
The acetyl group at the phenolic oxygen is critical for the irreversible inhibition of COX enzymes. Removal of this group yields salicylic acid, which is a reversible COX inhibitor with weaker anti-inflammatory activity. The carboxylic acid group is essential for binding to the arginine residue (Arg120) at the entrance of the COX active site channel.

Modifications to the aspirin scaffold:
- Diflunisal: Fluorinated biphenyl analog with enhanced anti-inflammatory potency but no antiplatelet activity
- Triflusal: Trifluoromethyl analog with more selective antiplatelet action
- Nitroaspirin (NCX-4016): Nitric oxide-donating aspirin with reduced GI toxicity
