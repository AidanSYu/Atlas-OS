Lipinski's Rule of Five: A Practical Guide for Drug Discovery Screening

1. HISTORICAL CONTEXT
In 1997, Christopher Lipinski and colleagues at Pfizer published a landmark analysis of the World Drug Index to identify physicochemical property ranges associated with oral drug absorption and permeability. The resulting "Rule of Five" (Ro5) has become the most widely used filter in early-stage drug discovery for assessing drug-likeness.

Reference: Lipinski CA, Lombardo F, Dominy BW, Feeney PJ. "Experimental and computational approaches to estimate solubility and permeability in drug discovery and development settings." Adv Drug Deliv Rev. 1997;23:3-25.

2. THE FOUR CRITERIA
A compound is likely to have poor oral absorption or permeability if it violates TWO OR MORE of the following criteria:

Rule 1 - Molecular Weight: MW <= 500 Da
Rationale: Larger molecules have reduced passive diffusion across biological membranes. The 500 Da cutoff corresponds roughly to the upper limit observed in orally bioavailable drugs.

Rule 2 - LogP (Lipophilicity): LogP <= 5 (often calculated as CLogP or ALogP)
Rationale: Compounds with high lipophilicity tend to have poor aqueous solubility, leading to dissolution-limited absorption. They also accumulate in lipid bilayers and show increased non-specific binding to off-target proteins.

Rule 3 - Hydrogen Bond Donors: HBD <= 5
Rationale: Hydrogen bond donors (OH and NH groups) must desolvate before crossing a lipid bilayer. Excessive HBDs create an energetic barrier to membrane permeation.

Rule 4 - Hydrogen Bond Acceptors: HBA <= 10
Rationale: Similar to HBDs, excessive hydrogen bond acceptors increase the desolvation penalty for membrane crossing. The 10-acceptor limit is approximately twice the donor limit, reflecting the lower energetic cost of desolvating acceptors.

3. THE "RULE OF FIVE" NAME
All cutoff values are multiples of five (500, 5, 5, 10), which is why Lipinski named it the "Rule of Five." This mnemonic has contributed significantly to its widespread adoption.

4. IMPORTANT CAVEATS
The Rule of Five has significant exceptions:
- Natural products and their analogs frequently violate Ro5 (cyclosporine, vancomycin)
- Compounds that are substrates for active transporters can be absorbed despite Ro5 violations
- The rules apply specifically to oral absorption via passive transcellular diffusion
- Antibiotics, antifungals, vitamins, and cardiac glycosides are enriched in Ro5 violators

5. BEYOND LIPINSKI: MODERN EXTENSIONS

Veber's Rules (2002):
- Rotatable bonds <= 10
- TPSA <= 140 Angstrom squared
These criteria were found to be better predictors of oral bioavailability in rats than Lipinski's rules alone.

The "Rule of Three" for Fragment-Based Drug Discovery (Congreve et al., 2003):
- MW <= 300
- LogP <= 3
- HBD <= 3
- HBA <= 3
Used as a starting point for fragment libraries in fragment-based lead discovery.

Beyond Rule of Five (bRo5) Space:
Modern analysis shows that drugs in "bRo5" chemical space (MW 500-1000, multiple Ro5 violations) can achieve oral bioavailability through:
- Macrocyclization (constraining conformation, e.g., cyclosporine)
- Intramolecular hydrogen bonding (chameleon molecules that change polarity based on environment)
- Cell-penetrating peptides

6. PRACTICAL APPLICATION IN ATLAS DISCOVERY OS
When the predict_properties tool returns molecular descriptors, Lipinski assessment is automatically computed:
- MolWt, LogP, HBD, HBA are the four primary Ro5 parameters
- TPSA provides Veber's extended assessment
- QED (Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness) integrates multiple property distributions from marketed drugs into a single 0-1 score

Interpretation Guidelines:
- QED > 0.67: High drug-likeness (favorable property profile)
- QED 0.49-0.67: Moderate drug-likeness (some concerns)
- QED < 0.49: Low drug-likeness (significant liabilities)

These thresholds are based on Bickerton et al. (2012) Nature Chemistry analysis of ~800 marketed oral drugs.

7. WORKED EXAMPLES

Aspirin (CC(=O)OC1=CC=CC=C1C(=O)O):
MW=180.16, LogP=1.19, HBD=1, HBA=4, TPSA=63.60
Lipinski: 0 violations (PASS)
QED: ~0.55 (moderate)

Ibuprofen (CC(C)CC1=CC=C(C=C1)C(C)C(=O)O):
MW=206.28, LogP=3.97, HBD=1, HBA=2, TPSA=37.30
Lipinski: 0 violations (PASS)
QED: ~0.72 (high)

Caffeine (CN1C=NC2=C1C(=O)N(C(=O)N2C)C):
MW=194.19, LogP=-0.07, HBD=0, HBA=6, TPSA=58.44
Lipinski: 0 violations (PASS)
QED: ~0.53 (moderate)

Cyclosporine A (a macrocyclic peptide, MW=1202.6):
MW=1202.6, LogP=2.92, HBD=5, HBA=23
Lipinski: 2 violations (MW, HBA) -> FAIL by Ro5
Yet cyclosporine IS orally bioavailable due to intramolecular H-bonding and chameleon behavior.
This illustrates that Ro5 is a guideline, not an absolute filter.
